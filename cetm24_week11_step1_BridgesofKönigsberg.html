The Bridges of Königsberg

Understanding the Birth of Graph Theory

The Problem

In the city of Königsberg (today Kaliningrad, Russia), there were four landmasses connected by seven bridges. The challenge: Can you walk through the city crossing each bridge exactly once?

Graph Representation

Euler abstracted the landmasses as vertices (A–D) and the bridges as edges (a–g).

Euler's Insight

The solution depends only on the degrees of the vertices:

  • A: degree 5
  • B: degree 3
  • C: degree 3
  • D: degree 3

A graph has:

  • Eulerian circuit if all vertices have even degree
  • Eulerian path if exactly 2 vertices have odd degree

Here: all 4 vertices are odd → No Eulerian walk exists.

Points to Note

  • Euler was first to solve such a problem using graphs.
  • By representing reality as a graph, puzzles become solvable by rules.
  • The existence (or non-existence) of a path does not depend on ingenuity.
  • It is a mathematical property of the graph itself.
  • Networks encode properties in their structure, defining behavior.
